Appendix A — Mathematical Savings Model

A.1 Definitions

Let:

T   = Tons processed per month
C   = Steel cost per ton
Sb  = Baseline scrap fraction (decimal form)
Sa  = Post-deployment scrap fraction (decimal form)
ΔS  = Sb − Sa  (Scrap improvement delta)

H   = Labor hours per ton (baseline)
ΔH  = Reduction in labor hours per ton
W   = Loaded labor rate per hour

A.2 Material Savings

Annual Material Savings (M) =
12 × T × C × ΔS

Constraint: 0 ≤ ΔS ≤ Sb

A.3 Labor Savings (Optional Input)

Annual Labor Savings (L) =
12 × T × ΔH × W

Constraint: ΔH ≥ 0

A.4 Total Modeled Value

Total Modeled Value (V) =
M + L

A.5 Subscription Capture Ratio

Let:

P = Annual Subscription Price
ρ = Capture Ratio

Then:

P = ρ × V

Where:

0.10 ≤ ρ ≤ 0.25 (typical operational range)

A.6 Sensitivity Analysis

Material savings scale linearly with tonnage and steel cost. The model therefore exhibits: 

∂M/∂T = 12 × C × ΔS
∂M/∂C = 12 × T × ΔS
∂M/∂ΔS = 12 × T × C

Pricing stability is preserved by conservative estimation of ΔS.

A.7 Benchmark Adjustment Factor

If percentile position within segment is known:

Adjusted Baseline Sb* =
Sb × (Segment Median / Facility Percentile Position)

Where Sb* ≥ 0

This adjustment is optional and only applied when statistically valid sample sizes exist.

A.8 Model Versioning

Each pricing agreement references:

  • Baseline Model Version ID
  • Delta Assumption Version ID
  • Benchmark Dataset Version ID (if applicable)

Model updates do not retroactively alter executed contracts.

Appendix B — Worked Numerical Example

This example illustrates the modeled value calculation using fixed inputs and conservative deltas. Values are provided for clarity of method, not as performance guarantees.

B.1 Example Inputs

Assume a commercial fabrication yard with:

T   = 60 tons/month
C   = $900/ton
Segment baseline scrap model: Sb = 0.055 (5.5%)
Conservative scrap improvement: ΔS = 0.015 (1.5 pts)

Optional labor inputs:
ΔH  = 0.10 hr/ton
W   = $85/hr

B.2 Annual Material Savings

M = 12 × T × C × ΔS
  = 12 × 60 × 900 × 0.015
  = 12 × 60 × 13.5
  = 12 × 810
  = $9,720 per year

B.3 Annual Labor Savings (Optional)

L = 12 × T × ΔH × W
  = 12 × 60 × 0.10 × 85
  = 12 × 60 × 8.5
  = 12 × 510
  = $6,120 per year

B.4 Total Modeled Value

V = M + L
  = 9,720 + 6,120
  = $15,840 per year

B.5 Indicative Subscription Range (Capture Ratio)

Using ρ in [0.12, 0.18]:

P_low  = 0.12 × V = 0.12 × 15,840 = $1,900.80
P_high = 0.18 × V = 0.18 × 15,840 = $2,851.20

Indicative annual subscription range: ~$1,900–$2,850

This range aligns with the lower commercial tiers. Higher-throughput facilities (larger T) scale linearly and therefore move into higher subscription bands under the same conservative delta assumptions.

B.6 Scale Illustration (Same Segment, Higher Throughput)

If T increases to 200 tons/month (all else unchanged):

M = 12 × 200 × 900 × 0.015 = $32,400/year
L = 12 × 200 × 0.10 × 85   = $20,400/year
V = $52,800/year

At ρ in [0.12, 0.18]:
P ≈ $6,336–$9,504 per year

This is an illustration of linear scaling. Real deployments may differ based on workflow complexity, bench count, and instrumentation scope.

Appendix D — Monte Carlo Sensitivity Simulation (Summary)

To evaluate pricing stability under uncertainty, a bounded Monte Carlo simulation framework can be applied to key variables: 

Variables simulated:

T   ~ Uniform[T_low, T_high]
C   ~ Uniform[C_low, C_high]
ΔS  ~ Uniform[ΔS_low, ΔS_high]
ΔH  ~ Uniform[ΔH_low, ΔH_high] (optional)

V = 12 × T × C × ΔS + 12 × T × ΔH × W
P = ρ × V

Distributions are bounded and conservative. No extreme-tail assumptions are used.

Simulation methodology follows bounded distribution modeling appropriate for operational risk estimation.[3]

Appendix F — Limitations & Assumptions

The savings model and pricing alignment framework described in this document rely on bounded assumptions. This section outlines structural limitations and explicit modeling constraints.

F.1 Baseline Assumptions

  • Industry scrap baselines represent median segment distributions, not facility-specific measurements.
  • Baseline values may vary regionally due to steel sourcing, supply chain variability, or labor conditions.
  • Self-reported scrap data is not used as a primary pricing input.

F.2 Delta Assumptions

  • Modeled scrap deltas (ΔS) assume disciplined workflow adoption.
  • Improvement deltas may not fully materialize without operator compliance.
  • Extremely high-efficiency facilities may experience smaller incremental gains.

F.3 Labor Modeling Constraints

  • Labor hour reduction (ΔH) is estimated and may vary by crew skill level.
  • Labor savings assume consistent workload; idle capacity is not monetized in the model.
  • Indirect labor impacts (supervision, scheduling overhead) are not included unless specified.

F.4 Market Variables

  • Steel cost (C) volatility directly impacts modeled material savings.
  • Tonnage variability (T) affects annualized calculations linearly.
  • Project mix shifts may alter scrap characteristics.

F.5 Monte Carlo Simulation Boundaries

  • Simulations use bounded uniform distributions for stability.
  • Extreme tail risk scenarios are intentionally excluded.
  • Simulation summaries are illustrative unless tied to real dataset releases.

F.6 ROI Comparison Limitations

  • Capital equipment ROI benchmarks vary by facility and financing structure.
  • Subscription ROI assumes operational continuity over a full annual period.
  • Comparison does not include tax treatment differences between CapEx and OpEx.

F.7 Model Governance

  • Model updates are versioned and do not retroactively modify executed agreements.
  • Benchmark index outputs require minimum anonymized sample thresholds.
  • Future refinements may adjust baseline medians as real operational data accumulates.

F.8 Non-Guarantee Statement

Modeled values represent structured estimates under defined assumptions. They are not guarantees of realized performance. Operational outcomes depend on adoption fidelity, workflow discipline, and environmental factors beyond the scope of the model.
